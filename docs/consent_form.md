### 1. Purpose
You are invited to participate in a **security-awareness exercise** that is part of an academic case study. The exercise measures the ability to recognize the visual and contextual signs of a fraudulent ("phishing") login page. Your participation helps the researchers understand how awareness training reduces human-factor risk.

### 2. What you will be asked to do
You will view one or more login pages — including a deliberately fake training page — and indicate which suspicious signs you can identify (for example, an incorrect web address, missing HTTPS/padlock, urgent wording, or spelling errors). The activity takes approximately **10–15 minutes**.

### 3. Data collected — and NOT collected
- **We collect only:** your anonymized participant ID, your *score* (how many suspicious signs you identified), and whether you have had prior security training.
- **We do NOT collect:** any password, username, email, or other credential you may type. The training page is a static mock-up that **discards all input** and stores nothing. No real account or system is involved.

### 4. Voluntary participation
Your participation is entirely voluntary. You may decline to answer any question or withdraw at any time without penalty. Withdrawal will result in your data being discarded.

### 5. Risks and benefits
There are no foreseeable risks beyond those of everyday computer use. The benefit is improved personal awareness of phishing techniques. The study benefits from anonymized, aggregated results.

### 6. Confidentiality
Results are reported only in aggregate (e.g., "X% of participants identified the URL anomaly"). No individual is identifiable in the thesis or any publication. Data is stored securely and used solely for this study.

### 7. Ethical scope
This exercise involves **no real systems, no live websites, and no credential capture**. It is conducted entirely within a controlled academic setting in compliance with institutional research-ethics requirements and applicable Philippine law (RA 10173, RA 10175).
